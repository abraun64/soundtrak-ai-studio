# 🔭 Aperture-setting

**Purpose**: reset the brand frame — land a new positioning, shift what the audience believes the brand stands for.

## When this mode is the right call

- KPI shape: brand awareness lift, consideration lift, "share of voice," cultural relevance, attitudinal-shift research
- Audience state: hold a stale or wrong belief about the brand; brand is invisible in the category conversation
- Brief signals: "we're repositioning," "we need to be famous for X," "we're losing relevance to <competitor>," "we want to be seen as <new attribute>"
- Stage of brand: relaunch, new leadership, post-acquisition, post-scandal, category expansion

## Exemplars in the repository

- **Apple — Think Different (1997)** — canonical brand-reset; positioned Apple's audience (not Apple) as the protagonists
- **IBM — Smarter Planet (2008)** — repositioned IBM from "boxes and services" to a thought-leader on civic infrastructure
- **Dove — Campaign for Real Beauty (2004)** — reset Dove's frame from "moisturising soap" to a stance on beauty itself
- **Nike — Dream Crazy / Kaepernick (2018)** — high-stakes aperture move that reset Nike's stance on cultural courage
- **Patagonia — Don't Buy This Jacket (2011)** — aperture set on environmental position; tested whether brand walks the talk

## What makes the mode work

- **Hero moment + supporting echo.** One asset carries the weight (a film, a manifesto, a stunt). The rest of the campaign amplifies, doesn't repeat.
- **A pointed POV, not a benefit claim.** Aperture campaigns stand for something the audience can agree or disagree with — that's where the cultural traction comes from.
- **Audience as protagonist.** Best aperture work makes the audience the hero of the brand's worldview — not the brand the hero.
- **Earned-media leverage.** The hero asset is designed to generate conversation; paid amplifies, doesn't carry.
- **Long tail.** Aperture campaigns reset the conversation for years — they don't expire when paid budget does.

## Anti-patterns

- **Aperture-as-puffery**: a generic "we believe in <unobjectionable thing>" film with no edge. The audience can't tell what changed.
- **Aperture-without-walk**: brand says it stands for X, the rest of the business doesn't reflect it. Audience smells the gap and the campaign accelerates distrust (Pepsi/Kendall Jenner is the textbook case).
- **Aperture-as-tactic**: trying to ladder up a tactical campaign to a brand statement. The KPI shape mismatch shows; the work feels grandiose for what it actually is.
- **Hero-only / no echo**: a single film with no supporting touchpoints. The moment lands, then evaporates because nothing reinforced it.

## When NOT to pick this mode

- The KPI is conversions-in-window — pick Direct-response
- The brand's frame is already correct; you just need more eyeballs — pick Participatory or Direct-response
- Existing audience is already loyal and the goal is to deepen — pick Identity reinforcement
- You can't fund both hero + echo — aperture without echo is wasted; pick a smaller mode you can fund fully
