# 🪞 Identity reinforcement

**Purpose**: deepen belonging with an existing audience. Touchpoints stitch a story the audience recognises themselves in. Mirrors, not funnels.

## When this mode is the right call

- KPI shape: NPS, retention, lifetime value, advocacy / referral rate, community engagement, churn reduction, employee engagement (for internal)
- Audience state: already loyal / already employed / already customer; goal is to deepen the bond, not acquire
- Brief signals: "loyalty programme," "advocacy," "customer marketing," "community," "employer brand," "we need our customers to be louder," "reduce churn by X"
- Stage of brand: established audience exists; the asset is to make them feel more *themselves* in the brand

## Exemplars in the repository

- **Patagonia — Worn Wear (2013+)** — reframed used Patagonia as more authentic than new; identity reinforcement through values
- **Apple — Shot on iPhone (2015+)** — customers as the protagonists; phone-owners become the brand's gallery
- **REI — #OptOutside (2015+)** — closed stores on Black Friday; co-op members felt more like members
- **Lego — Adult Fans of Lego (AFOL) recognition** — turned a sub-segment from "customers" into "community" with its own canon
- **Sephora — Beauty Insider community** — turned the loyalty programme into a place to *be*, not just earn points
- **Airbnb — "Made Possible by Hosts" / "We Accept"** — stitched the host identity into the brand's own
- **Glossier — customer-as-marketing (DTC era)** — customers' bathroom shelfies became the campaign

## What makes the mode work

- **Audience as protagonist** — same lever as Aperture-setting, but pointed at the existing audience, not a new one
- **Recognition over persuasion** — the audience isn't being sold to; they're being *seen*. "You're not crazy for caring about this; we see you."
- **Insider language and detail** — references, in-jokes, deep specifics that signal "we know our people." Outsiders may not get it; that's a feature.
- **Status conferral** — being in the campaign (literally or figuratively) elevates the audience's status in their community
- **Long-tail compound interest** — identity-reinforcement work compounds; each campaign deepens the moat the brand's already built

## Anti-patterns

- **Faux-community**: brand declares a "community" that doesn't actually exist or have its own life. Audiences sniff this immediately.
- **Generic appreciation**: "thank you for being a customer" emails that anyone could send. Specificity is everything in this mode.
- **Identity LARP**: brand performs an identity it doesn't deserve (eco-brand selling fast-fashion volumes, "we're family" from a company that just did layoffs).
- **Insider too inside**: in-jokes so deep that even mid-loyalty customers feel locked out. There's a line between "feels like home" and "feels exclusionary."
- **Confusing identity-reinforcement with acquisition**: building a campaign designed to deepen loyalty, then judging it on new-customer KPIs. The instrument doesn't fit the metric.

## When NOT to pick this mode

- Existing audience is too small to be the campaign's primary mover — pick a mode that grows the audience first
- Brand is too new to have an "existing audience" identity to reinforce — pick Aperture or Participatory
- KPI is acquisition / new customers / awareness — wrong instrument
- The brand-audience relationship is transactional (commodity, low-affinity) — identity-reinforcement requires an identity worth reinforcing
- Internal/employer-brand variant: only works if the underlying employee experience supports the claim — otherwise the campaign accelerates cynicism
