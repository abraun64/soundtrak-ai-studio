## Big idea
Allbirds built its brand around sustainable materials (merino wool, eucalyptus, sugarcane foam) and made the sustainability legible at every touchpoint — most distinctively by adding a carbon footprint number to every product, like a calorie count on food.

## Why it worked
- Concrete metric (kg CO₂e per pair) beat vague sustainability claims everyone else was making
- Carbon-label format borrowed an established mental model (nutrition labels) — instantly legible
- Material story (sugar cane! eucalyptus! merino!) gave the audience something specific to repeat to friends
- Identity-reinforcement for environmentally-conscious buyers without preaching
- Aperture-secondary: pressured competitors to disclose their own numbers; reframed category expectations

## What a CD should steal
- Make abstract claims concrete with a number + a unit — "sustainable" is invisible; "7.3 kg CO₂e" is legible
- Borrow a familiar mental model (nutrition labels) to make a novel claim immediately understandable
- Materials story as content — specifics ("eucalyptus pulp from FSC-certified forests") beat adjectives
- Transparency as competitive pressure — once you publish, competitors must respond

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Greenwash exposure — Allbirds was sued for misleading sustainability claims, which dented credibility
- Metric-without-meaning — if the audience can't compare your number to anything, it's noise
- Sustainability as the only story — Allbirds had quality and brand problems beyond materials, and the sustainability story couldn't carry them
- Disclosure trap — once you've published, you must keep publishing; transparency that lapses becomes a credibility liability
