## Big idea
Steve Jobs returned to a near-bankrupt Apple and reset the brand's frame from "computer maker" to "tool for the people who change the world." A 60-second manifesto film celebrated misfits, rebels, and troublemakers — historical figures from Einstein to Gandhi to Picasso — and ended with a small Apple logo. No product appeared.

## Why it worked
- The audience was the protagonist, not Apple. Buyers saw themselves in the manifesto, not the product.
- Pointed POV ("the people who think they can change the world are the ones who do") gave the audience something to agree with — a stake.
- Hero film + supporting OOH echo carried for years on minimal media weight because earned media did the work.
- Internal alignment effect: the campaign re-recruited Apple's own employees to a story worth working for.

## What a CD should steal
- The structural move of making the audience the hero of the brand's worldview.
- The discipline of zero product in the hero asset — let the worldview do the heavy lifting.
- Hero + echo architecture: one asset carries the weight, the rest amplifies without repeating.
- Manifesto as a format — use it when you genuinely have a point of view, not as a template.

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Manifesto-without-POV: a film that gestures at meaning without taking a position. Audiences smell it.
- Imitation: countless "here's to the X" rip-offs have followed; if the device is recognisable, you've cloned the surface.
- Aperture without a business behind it: Apple had the iMac queued to back the claim. A reset with no product/operational backup accelerates distrust.
