## Big idea
Burger King used geofencing to detect when a user opened the BK app inside (or within 600 feet of) a McDonald's, then unlocked a 1-cent Whopper redeemable only at the nearest BK. To participate, you had to physically walk into a McDonald's.

## Why it worked
- The "you have to act to get the joke" structure made participation the proof — a tweet about it isn't the campaign; *going* is
- Used competitor's own stores as the stage — McDonald's couldn't respond without elevating BK
- Tied to a real business outcome (app downloads) that had been failing — wasn't just a stunt
- Free PR — every news outlet covered the audacity; paid amplification was minimal
- Worked across the funnel: aperture (cultural moment) → participation (geofence trigger) → direct-response (app install + offer redemption)

## What a CD should steal
- Stunt-with-utility — the "joke" must deliver a real outcome (free Whopper, app install, measurable conversion)
- Use competitor or category convention as the stage — when others can't respond without elevating you, you've designed it right
- Force physical action — physical participation generates 10x the social currency of a click
- Bake the business outcome into the participation mechanic from concept stage

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Stunt-without-utility: clever, no payoff, audience feels played
- Geofence theatre without scale: works in 5 markets, useless as a campaign at national scale
- Competitor-baiting without category permission: only the #2 or scrappy challenger can punch up; market leaders look mean
- Underpowered tech: if the geofence fails, the joke fails — production risk is real
