## Big idea
Cadbury built a web platform where you could "hide" a Cadbury Easter egg anywhere in the world (pin it on a map), write a personal clue, and send the link to a loved one. The recipient followed the clue to the location; Cadbury delivered the egg there.

## Why it worked
- Reframed Easter egg hunt from a backyard ritual to a global, distance-collapsing gesture — perfect for lockdown / diaspora moment
- The participation was a gift to someone else, not self-directed — emotional stakes higher than typical participation
- Map-as-asset — the world map filled with pins was its own ambient campaign
- Logistics-as-creative — Cadbury actually delivering eggs to pinned locations was the proof
- Combined Cadbury's Easter equity (existing ritual) with a new technology layer

## What a CD should steal
- Participation-as-gift — when participation involves giving to someone else, the emotional weight (and share rate) jumps
- Marry digital mechanic with physical fulfilment — the friction of physical delivery is what gives the gesture meaning
- Layer new tech onto an existing brand ritual rather than inventing a new one — Cadbury already owned Easter
- Map-as-aggregate-asset — individual participations stack into a campaign-scale visual

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Logistics break the campaign — if delivery fails, the campaign is the failure
- Tech-driven gimmick without emotional spine: same mechanic for a generic product would feel empty
- Scale ceiling: physical fulfilment caps how many people can participate; needs a graceful fail mode for over-demand
