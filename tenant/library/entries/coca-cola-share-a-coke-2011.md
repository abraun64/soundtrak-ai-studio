## Big idea
Coca-Cola replaced its iconic logo on bottles and cans with 150 common first names, then later added relationships ("Mum," "Best Friend") and other categories. The audience went hunting for their name, gifted bottles with friends' names, and posted finds to social.

## Why it worked
- Personalisation at scale — turned a commodity bottle into a personal object
- Gift mechanic — bottles became giftable in low-stakes everyday contexts ("here's a Coke with your name on it")
- Search mechanic — the "find your name" hunt drove store visits and category share
- Social-share engine — every found bottle was photographed; UGC carried the campaign organically
- Reversibility: a packaging change reads as bigger commitment than an ad — and Coca-Cola sacrificed its sacred logo to make it work

## What a CD should steal
- Packaging is media — for product brands, the packaging-as-asset move is often higher-impact than any ad campaign
- Personalisation at scale via shared categories (names, cities, identities) — works when the database is finite enough to print but personal enough to feel custom
- Gift mechanic — design the asset to be given, not just owned
- Sacrifice an iconic element — what would your brand have to give up to earn the audience's attention?

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Personalisation theatre: "your name on it" via thin sticker — the audience knows the difference between sacrificed-real-estate and gimmick
- Name list politics: who's *not* on the list becomes the story (some markets had backlash for missing common ethnic names)
- One-and-done: Coca-Cola has had to re-invent the platform multiple times (relationships, cities, songs) to keep it fresh
- Untested at scale: requires manufacturing capability to run hundreds of SKUs — easier for major brands than challengers
