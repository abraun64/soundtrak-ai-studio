## Big idea
Dollar Shave Club's founder Michael Dubin walked through the company's warehouse on camera, deadpanned a script about how razor blade prices are absurd, threw a bear-suited employee around, and ended with the URL. Total production cost: ~$4,500.

## Why it worked
- Founder-led credibility — the audience saw the actual person responsible, not an actor
- One asset, one URL, one offer — frictionless funnel
- Tone (irreverent, self-aware, profane) immediately differentiated from Gillette's polished sincerity
- Distributed natively as a video, not a TV ad — designed for share, not slot
- Massive arbitrage: when the cost-to-distinction ratio is this favourable, even a single asset can launch a category disruptor
- Aperture secondary: reframed shaving as a subscription category, not a retail-aisle category

## What a CD should steal
- Founder-led video can outperform $5M production when the founder is comfortable on camera and the story is real
- Tone discontinuity within a sleepy category creates instant memory and share
- Single-URL DR funnel — no menu, no exploration, one button
- Designed for native-platform distribution — vertical, scrollable, screenshot-worthy
- Launch with a wedge, not a brand — DSC could expand to other categories later because the wedge worked

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Founder-on-camera without founder-charisma: forced, awkward, kills the credibility
- Tone-imitation without underlying disruption: irreverence-as-veneer collapses when the product is just average
- One-hit wonder risk: DSC struggled to sustain the brand voice after the launch moment; took years to figure out the second act
- Category-permission: DSC could disrupt razors because razors were a flagrant ripoff; not every category supports the "ripoff" framing
