## Big idea
Dove broke from category convention (perfect, retouched, aspirational beauty) by featuring real women of varied ages, sizes, ethnicities, and shapes — not models. Backed by proprietary research on women's self-perception. Multi-decade platform: Evolution (2006), Real Beauty Sketches (2013), #ShowUs (2019), and many more.

## Why it worked
- Took a real position against the entire category's playbook — gave Dove a stake the audience could agree with
- Research-led (not just creative): Dove published the "Real Beauty" study showing 2% of women called themselves beautiful, which earned the campaign editorial cover
- Multi-decade compounding: each new execution deepens the brand's claim rather than restarting
- Earned-media engine: every execution generated talkability; PR did the work paid couldn't have funded
- Walked the talk: zero-retouching commitment, casting policy changes — credibility of action backed the words

## What a CD should steal
- Counter-category positioning: when the whole category does X, the brand that does ¬X owns a stake nobody else can claim
- Pair creative with proprietary research — research gives the campaign editorial credibility paid can't buy
- Multi-execution platform vs. one-off campaign — each execution adds capital to the original claim
- Walk-the-talk discipline — the operational commitments are what make the claim credible

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Issue-as-veneer: take a stance, don't change the product or operations — accelerates distrust (Unilever's own portfolio includes Axe; that contradiction has been called out for years)
- One-and-done: a single "real beauty" film without the platform commitment — feels opportunistic
- Borrowed authority: brands without category permission attempting Dove-style stance — feels cynical
- The Pepsi/Kendall problem: any brand taking a stance must be ready to fund the operational/category change, not just the film
