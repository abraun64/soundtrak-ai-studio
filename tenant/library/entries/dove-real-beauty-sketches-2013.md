## Big idea
Dove hired an FBI forensic artist to draw women based on (1) their own self-description and (2) a stranger's description of them. The two sketches were dramatically different — the stranger's was always more beautiful. The film became one of the most-shared ads ever.

## Why it worked
- Social-experiment format gave the campaign the feel of documentary truth, not advertising
- Visualised an emotional insight (women see themselves more harshly than others see them) that women instantly recognised
- Reveal moment was structured for emotional payoff — pacing engineered for share
- Slotted into Dove's existing Real Beauty platform — every previous execution made this one credible
- The format was new, but the brand stance was old — viewers trusted Dove's right to make the film

## What a CD should steal
- Social-experiment format converts emotional insight into shareable proof
- Reveal moment as creative architecture — build to a payoff that earns the share
- Use partners with editorial credibility (FBI sketch artist, scientists) to lend the format real-world weight
- Land new executions on top of established platforms — borrowed credibility compounds
- Visualise the insight rather than describe it — show, don't tell, at brand-platform scale

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Social-experiment-as-template: many imitators ("scientists tested" / "hidden camera" formats) feel formulaic now
- Manufactured-reveal: if the reveal feels scripted, the whole film unravels
- Methodology critique: some critics argued the campaign's setup was selection-biased; brands using "research" should be ready to defend it
- Emotional manipulation backlash — overly engineered emotional reveals can trigger pushback as audiences grow more media-literate
