## Big idea
Drift (a chatbot SaaS) named and codified "Conversational Marketing" — the practice of replacing lead-gen forms with real-time chat conversations. CEO David Cancel wrote the book; the company published a framework, ran a conference, and produced a content engine around the methodology. Drift became the default Conversational Marketing vendor by definition.

## Why it worked
- Same playbook as HubSpot's inbound: name a category, write the book, ship the tool, own the practice
- Founder-led content (CEO + executive bench) gave the methodology authority faster than corporate content would have
- Solved a real B2B buyer frustration (the form-to-MQL-to-SDR-cycle was widely hated) — methodology landed because the audience already knew the pain
- Tight loop: book → framework → product features → certification — every layer reinforced the others
- Compressed timeline: from launch to category leadership in ~3 years, faster than HubSpot's inbound arc

## What a CD should steal
- Methodology-as-marketing in B2B works fast when the audience already feels the underlying pain
- Founder as primary content vehicle — accelerates authority but creates founder-dependence
- Pair a book with a framework with a tool — the three together make the category claim defensible
- Name the practice in a way that's portable to LinkedIn / job titles / conference tracks — "Conversational Marketing" became a real role

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Category-without-pain: if the buyer doesn't already feel the problem, no methodology can manufacture demand
- Founder-dependent voice: when David Cancel stepped back, Drift's voice noticeably shifted
- Category gets absorbed: Conversational Marketing as a standalone category was eventually subsumed into broader "revenue engagement" — methodology categories have a lifespan
- Methodology theatre: ship a framework, never update it — competitors leapfrog with v2
