## Big idea
Figma's growth motion centred designers as the protagonists: an annual conference (Config) that became *the* designer-community gathering, a plugin ecosystem that empowered designers to extend the tool, free education content, and a brand voice that treated designers as the audience worth respecting.

## Why it worked
- Identity-reinforcement: "I'm a Figma designer" became professional identity, sticky in a way feature-comparisons aren't
- Conference (Config) gave the audience a place to gather in person — accelerated adoption via peer signal
- Plugin ecosystem turned advanced users into co-creators with economic alignment — flywheel turns
- Free education for designers + brand voice that respected craft generated word-of-mouth in a tight community
- Won the market because the *community* moved together — design teams adopted Figma when their peers did, not when sales pitched
- Educational pipeline secondary: Figma's content engine taught design practice generally, not just product use

## What a CD should steal
- Community-as-marketing in B2B-prosumer: the audience already talks to each other; make sure they're talking about you
- Conference as community-formation, not lead-gen — the second-order pipeline impact is bigger than the booth-meeting impact
- Plugin / ecosystem economics give power users a reason to stay and recruit
- Free education content positions the brand as the practice's caretaker, not just a vendor
- Respect-the-audience tone in B2B is criminally underrated — designers/developers/data-people all reward it disproportionately

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Community-without-listening: claim community-led, ignore community feedback — backlash is fast and brutal
- Conference-as-sales-funnel: optimised purely for sales meetings hollows the community signal
- Ecosystem governance: plugin economies require ongoing curation; the unmaintained ones become liabilities
- Acquisition risk: post-Adobe acquisition uncertainty (and later block) created identity questions for the community
