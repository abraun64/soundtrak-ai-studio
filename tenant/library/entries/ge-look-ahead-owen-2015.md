## Big idea
GE faced a talent problem: top software engineers didn't think of GE as a software employer (it was perceived as an industrial dinosaur). The campaign followed Owen, a fresh CS grad hired by GE, trying to explain to his family and friends that yes, GE actually writes code — and getting baffled, eye-rolling reactions. The comedic discomfort *was* the brand-reframing argument.

## Why it worked
- Tackled a recruitment problem (perception gap) with brand-marketing tools at brand-marketing scale
- Comedic format gave a heavy B2B brand permission to feel light and self-aware
- Audience felt seen: the joke landed because young engineers *did* feel weird telling people they worked at GE
- Combined aperture (broad public reframing) with practical funnel (recruitment-site traffic + application volume)
- Acknowledged the brand's perception problem honestly — earned credibility by not pretending the gap didn't exist

## What a CD should steal
- Recruitment campaigns can do brand work — talent perception and customer perception overlap
- Use the audience's own ambivalence as the joke — earns the right to reframe
- Self-aware brand voice in B2B (when warranted) can break category convention
- Single character / story format can carry a multi-execution platform without feeling fragmented

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Self-aware-as-cute: when the self-awareness has no underlying change to back it up, reads as deflection
- Character-dependent: when the actor moves on, the platform can stall
- Niche audience (engineers) requires niche cultural references; broad audience watching this campaign mostly didn't get it — that's fine, but means the ROI is in the niche, not the broad
