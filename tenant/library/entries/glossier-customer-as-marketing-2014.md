## Big idea
Emily Weiss launched Into the Gloss (a beauty blog) in 2010 before launching Glossier (the brand) in 2014. By the time Glossier existed, it had a media audience that wanted to be customers — and Glossier built its product line in dialogue with that audience, then turned customers into the marketing through bathroom-shelfie Instagram and Community Reps.

## Why it worked
- Audience-first sequence — built a media platform with editorial credibility, then introduced a product to its audience
- Customer-shaped product — Glossier launched with a milky cleanser because the audience kept asking for one; co-creation as marketing
- Instagram-native aesthetic — pink + millennial pastels + bathroom counters → instantly recognisable in-feed
- UGC engine: Community Reps got commission for posts; customers posted unprompted because the product was photogenic
- Identity-reinforcement at scale — "I'm a Glossier girl" became a recognisable identity, not just a shopping preference
- The brand voice (warm, conspiratorial, "us girls") was consistent across owned, paid, and customer-generated channels

## What a CD should steal
- Build the audience before the product — media-first strategy de-risks the launch
- Co-create the product with the audience and let them tell the story — they were there; they know
- Aesthetic-as-IP — a consistent visual identity that's instantly recognisable in the audience's social feeds
- Make products that photograph well — the packaging is the campaign in social-native categories
- Tier the participation: passive customers post sometimes; community reps post for commission; superfans post for status

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Audience-first inversion: media-first only works if the media itself is genuinely good — most brand blogs are not
- Aesthetic-cargo-cult: millennial pink was widely copied to the point of becoming a category cliché
- Community-rep economy: when influencer commissions become the dominant marketing channel, costs scale linearly
- Brand-voice fatigue: Glossier's voice felt fresh in 2014 and dated by 2022; voices age
- Product-line discipline: easy to overextend when the audience is loyal; harder to maintain quality across categories
