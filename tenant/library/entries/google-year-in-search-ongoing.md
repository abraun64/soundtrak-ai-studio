## Big idea
Every December, Google produces a 2-3 minute film stitching together the year's most-searched questions, terms, and moments — set to music, with archival footage. The film doubles as a brand reminder that Google holds the world's questions, and a tribute to whatever the year was emotionally.

## Why it worked
- Data Google was already collecting became the campaign's source material — zero incremental data investment
- Pure emotional summary — no product pitch, no CTA, just the year reflected back
- Annual cadence makes it a Q4 cultural moment — competitors can't pre-empt; the date is owned
- Reinforces Google's core utility (asking questions) without explaining or selling it
- Reframes search data from "surveillance" to "shared human record" — defensive narrative against privacy criticism
- Multi-decade compounding — each year's film adds to the platform's authority

## What a CD should steal
- Annual cadence as a platform — own a date in the calendar
- Source material from data you already have — repurpose, don't manufacture
- Pure-emotion summary without pitching — restraint reads as confidence
- Reframe a defensive narrative (privacy, surveillance, addiction) by showing the positive use of the data
- Multi-decade thinking — build a platform that compounds annually rather than restart yearly

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Sentimentality without specificity: generic "what a year" reels feel hollow
- Data-without-curation: raw search trends include ugliness; the curation is the craft
- Defensive narrative limits: the platform softens but doesn't eliminate privacy criticism
- Annual cadence creates pressure: years where culture feels heavy or unresolved (2020, 2024) make the format harder to land
