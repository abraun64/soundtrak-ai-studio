## Strategic spine
- **Audience insight**: clinicians are drowning in admin and burning out; they've always used dark humour to survive the hard days. Health-system leaders face a workforce shortfall you can't train your way out of (WHO: ~11M shortfall by 2030) — the only lever left is giving the people already there their time back.
- **Big idea**: give clinicians **relief — on repeat** (time, capacity, headspace back), dramatised by hijacking the visual + verbal codes of pharma advertising.
- **Key message**: "Side effects may include: relief, on repeat" — Heidi as the *AI Care Partner* that hands the day back.
- **Product truth**: AI clinical documentation expanding to "tackles every clinical task" (scribe + coding + care-gap outreach + comms).
- **Channel mix**: a brand film ("Side Effects May Include…") + a homepage hero takeover fronted by "Dr Steve" + a founder-signed blog manifesto, rolling out over months.

## Creative move
**What it did**: turned a dry, serious, compliance-heavy B2B category (clinical AI) into a brand platform by **satirising pharmaceutical advertising** — the reassuring silver-haired doctor ("Dr Steve") in his sunlit diploma-lined office, the "side effects may include", the prescribing/warning grammar — all flipped optimistic.

**The move** (portable):
- **Single-minded proposition, on repeat** — one benefit ("relief"), threaded through every execution and literally named in the line.
- **Hijack an adjacent category's codes** — borrow the instantly-recognisable grammar of pharma ads and subvert it. Familiarity + violation = attention you don't have to buy.
- **A recurring character as a brand asset** — "Dr Steve", the archetypal trusted-doctor spokesperson, gives the platform an ownable, memorable face to run for months.
- **Humour that earns the right** — the satire is justified by an audience truth (clinicians cope with humour) and a credible messenger (the founder is a former clinician), so it lands as empathy, not mockery.
- **Proof under the joke** — hard Impact-Report stats (59 min/day, 97% good/excellent, 2.6M visits/week, enterprise logos) keep a bold idea grounded and believable.
- **A repeatable copy engine** — "Side effects may include: ___" generates endless on-brand lines ("Remember daylight?", "You didn't go to med school to become a paperwork pusher", "Chief Medical Officer, not Chief Admin Officer").

**Why it worked**:
- Category-code hijack buys instant comprehension *and* a pattern-interrupt (familiar frame + rule-break = attention + memory).
- Humour on a painful in-group topic signals "we get you" → belonging with clinicians.
- A single, human benefit ("relief") is ownable where a feature list in a crowded AI category is not.
- Founder credibility + real proof inoculate against "making light of a serious problem".

**When to steal**:
- Dry / serious / technical B2B category (AI, security, compliance, fintech, healthcare) where everyone sounds identical → humour + a hijacked category code cuts through.
- The benefit is abstract (time back, headspace, peace of mind) → dramatise it inside a familiar cultural frame the audience decodes instantly.
- Your audience has an in-group culture (clinicians, engineers, operators) → meet them with their own humour, and earn it with a credible messenger.
- You need a platform that sustains — a repeatable headline engine + a recurring character beat a one-off film.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Satire without audience truth or a credible messenger reads as tone-deaf — making light of real pain.
- A hijacked category code with no proof underneath is a joke with nothing behind it.
- Regulated-category risk — the pharma pastiche must not imply medical claims; keep every stat real and sourced.
- A character that outshines the product; the joke must always ladder back to the benefit.

## Visual direction
Pharma-commercial pastiche played straight: the warm, sunlit executive doctor's office, framed diplomas, a distinguished silver-haired physician in a white coat ("Dr Steve"), a brass desk nameplate. Premium film grade, calm and gently absurd. Cream editorial serif for the hero line **"Relief, on repeat."** The site runs it as a full hero takeover; the film mimics a TV pharma spot ("Prescribed viewing for anyone suffering from healthcare inefficiency").
