## Big idea
Rethink Canada asked people from around the world to "draw ketchup." The vast majority drew a Heinz-shaped bottle, a Heinz-shaped label, the Heinz keystone label, even the "57." The campaign was the resulting wall of drawings, presented without commentary.

## Why it worked
- Used the audience to prove the brand claim — Heinz didn't say it was the default ketchup; the audience proved it
- Almost zero production cost — the participation *was* the asset
- The insight was the campaign — no clever line needed; the proof carried the meaning
- Earned-media engine — academic-feeling proof generated editorial coverage that paid couldn't have funded
- Internationally legible — drawings work in every language

## What a CD should steal
- Find proof the audience can *demonstrate*, not just consume
- Low-tech, high-distinction: the format (collected drawings) is the medium
- Let the proof speak — resist the urge to write a clever line over the audience's own work
- Use the participation device to also be the asset library (each drawing is a unit you can re-publish)

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Forced proof: if the audience *wouldn't* spontaneously default to your brand, this format collapses — you can't fake the result
- Over-explanation: a brand instinct to add a clever headline or voiceover would have killed the campaign
- Repeat-performance fatigue: once a brand has done a "draw-it" campaign, the second one feels formulaic
