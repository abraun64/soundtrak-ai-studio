## Big idea
HubSpot's founders coined "inbound marketing" as a category (vs. outbound / interruption), wrote the book on it, built a free certification programme that taught it, and ran an annual event (INBOUND) that gathered the practitioners — all of whom became HubSpot users by default.

## Why it worked
- Category creation gave HubSpot a frame in which it was definitionally the leader
- Education-as-marketing — practitioners trained on the methodology naturally evaluated HubSpot when buying
- Certification gave the audience a credential (LinkedIn-shareable) — status currency turned learners into evangelists
- Compounding content engine — every blog post, certification module, and event talk added to the methodology's authority
- Pipeline impact ran for 15+ years — buying committees were full of HubSpot-certified people by the time RFPs came up

## What a CD should steal
- Invent a category, don't compete in one — the brand that names the practice owns the practice
- Free certification as a long-term moat — credentialled practitioners are evangelists for life
- Framework-as-IP — concepts like "the flywheel," "the funnel," "MQL/SQL" became HubSpot's vocabulary in the industry
- Multi-decade thinking — pipeline ROI shows up over years, not quarters
- Pair a methodology, a credential, and a conference — the three-legged stool of category leadership

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Category-creation-without-substance: claim a category, ship a thin framework — competitors and analysts will dismantle it
- Certification mill: too easy a certification dilutes value; too hard a certification kills volume
- Content treadmill: when every competitor is producing inbound content, the original loses share unless it keeps innovating the methodology
- Founder-thought-leader risk: tying the methodology to a specific founder voice creates dependence
