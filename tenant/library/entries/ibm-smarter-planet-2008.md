## Big idea
IBM was being seen as a legacy hardware-and-services vendor in a moment when buyers (CIOs, CEOs, civic leaders) needed a thought partner for the digital transformation of the physical world. Smarter Planet repositioned IBM as the company that helps the world's systems — traffic grids, food supply chains, hospitals, power — get smarter, with IBM technology as the substrate.

## Why it worked
- Reset IBM's frame from "vendor" to "thought leader on civic infrastructure" — moved the conversation up the C-suite
- Audience was the protagonist: cities, hospitals, supply chain leaders saw themselves as the heroes; IBM was the enabler
- Multi-year, multi-execution platform — every quarter brought a new "smarter X" execution, all under one frame
- Targeted thought-leadership content (white papers, executive talks, conferences) gave sales teams credibility at the C-suite level
- Tied to a real business shift inside IBM (away from hardware, toward services + analytics) — operations backed the claim

## What a CD should steal
- B2B aperture is real and high-ROI — repositioning at the C-suite drives multi-year buying decisions
- Audience-as-protagonist works in B2B (more than it gets credit for) — the buyer is making *their* career bet, not IBM's
- Single frame, infinite executions — pick a frame elastic enough to hold years of executions
- Pair the aperture campaign with sales-enablement content engine — the salesforce becomes the second act of the campaign
- Operational alignment — the brand claim must match the business pivot

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Frame-without-operations: IBM's claim was credible because IBM was actually pivoting; a B2B aperture without business pivot is theatre
- Vague-thought-leadership: "smarter planet" feels meaty because every execution was a concrete civic case; the abstraction needs concrete proof
- Campaign-fatigue: multi-year platforms need genuine new executions, not just refresh — Smarter Planet eventually wound down because the executions started repeating
