## Big idea
IKEA built one of the first consumer ARKit apps: point your phone at your room, drop a true-to-scale IKEA piece into the scene, walk around it, decide if it fits. The app is the campaign.

## Why it worked
- Solved a genuine purchase-decision problem (does this fit, does it look right) that had previously blocked online furniture buying
- Tech-as-marketing — being early on a platform (ARKit launch) was itself the story; Apple co-promoted
- Removed the highest-friction step of online furniture purchase → direct conversion lift
- Utility-led, not promotion-led — the app earned downloads on its own merit, not via ad spend

## What a CD should steal
- Tool-as-campaign — when the brand can build a utility the audience would actually use, the utility outperforms any ad about the product
- First-mover on a platform — platform launches reward brands willing to be the showcase use case
- Solve a category-wide friction with tech — every furniture buyer had this problem; IKEA owned the solution
- Co-promotion with platform owners — partnering with Apple at ARKit launch got distribution paid couldn't have bought

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Tech-for-tech's-sake: AR app where the AR isn't actually solving anything — gets downloaded, opened once, deleted
- Built-then-abandoned: ship the tool, never update — users churn, app becomes a museum piece
- Solves a problem nobody has: building an app for a step the audience doesn't actually find painful
