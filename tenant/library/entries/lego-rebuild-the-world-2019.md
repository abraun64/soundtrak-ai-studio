## Big idea
LEGO repositioned the brand from "the toy that builds things" to "the tool the next generation rebuilds the world with." The hero films featured kids' creative chaos building impossible objects, animals, and inventions — celebrating creative-problem-solving as the brand's contribution to childhood.

## Why it worked
- Reframed the brand's frame from object (bricks) to capability (creative problem-solving)
- Played to parents AND children simultaneously — parents value creativity-development; kids value playful chaos
- Multi-year platform with many executions — annual rotation kept the frame fresh
- Aligned with LEGO's product portfolio (Architecture, Technic, Friends, etc.) — frame is elastic enough to hold the whole catalogue
- Cultural-relevance secondary effect — LEGO became the brand parents could feel virtuous buying

## What a CD should steal
- Reframe from object to capability — what does your product *enable* in the audience's life?
- Dual-audience design — when buyer and user are different, the campaign needs both
- Long-running platform that holds the whole portfolio — pick a frame elastic enough
- Generational framing — "the next generation" is durable because it's continually replenished

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Frame-too-broad: "creativity" can become a non-claim (every brand says it); LEGO has product depth to back it up, lesser brands don't
- Aspirational-without-product-fit: claim capability the product can't really deliver
- Adult-LEGO drift: LEGO's adult-collector audience (AFOL) isn't well served by the "next generation" frame; separate platforms emerged
- Multi-year platform fatigue — needs visible execution evolution to stay alive
