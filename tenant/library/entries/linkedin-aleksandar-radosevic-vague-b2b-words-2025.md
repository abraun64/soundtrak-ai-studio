## Strategic spine

**Audience insight**: B2B SaaS marketers know their copy is vague but don't have a sharp diagnostic for *why* — they default to "we need to be clearer" without knowing what to swap. They want a rule, not a sentiment.
**Big idea**: Vague B2B words describe how you want the product to be *perceived* — not what the product actually *does*. Replace perception-words with product-words and copy gets clearer without getting longer.
**Key message**: Identify your perception-words → diagnose what concrete product detail they're hiding → swap. Five-step framework + a copy-edit prompt.
**Product or service**: The author's B2B-SaaS copywriting consultancy (implied, not pitched directly).
**Channel mix**: LinkedIn organic (primary), soft DM CTA for direct consult inquiries

## Creative move

**What it did**: Tight diagnostic-framework LinkedIn post that names a specific copywriting failure mode ("perception-words instead of product-words"), gives a 5-step framework to fix it, hands the reader a copy-edit prompt, and closes with a soft DM CTA — no pitch turn, no aggressive CTA, no image. Pure craft post.

**The move** (bullets):
- **One-line diagnostic that reframes the category problem**: "vague B2B words describe how you want the product to be PERCEIVED, not what it DOES" — names the failure in a sentence the reader can use immediately. The perception-vs-product distinction is the load-bearing IP.
- **Numbered framework (1–5)**: gives the reader a procedure, not just a principle. Procedures travel further than principles on LinkedIn.
- **Free prompt as the deliverable**: same move as Russell's post — give the reader a copy-pastable tool that produces value in the next 5 minutes.
- **No-image, copy-only**: deliberate. The post argues for clarity and demonstrates it by skipping graphic crutches. Form mirrors content.
- **Soft DM CTA**: "If you need help, just DM me. Happy to take a look :)" — no urgency, no scarcity. Lower conversion ceiling, higher trust ceiling.

**Why it worked** (bullets):
- The perception-vs-product reframe is a real diagnostic — readers can apply it to their own homepage in 30 seconds and feel the click.
- Numbered frameworks earn comments because readers debate the order / steps.
- No-image posts perform well on LinkedIn for craft-creator accounts — text-only reads as "expertise post" not "content marketing."
- Soft CTA + smaller follower base + niche topic = the comments are likely high-intent (potential clients diagnosing themselves, not engagement farmers).

**When to steal**:
- LinkedIn posts where the topic IS the craft (writing about copy, design about design, content about content) — form-mirrors-content is a free trust signal.
- When you have a real, sharable diagnostic frame (a 5-step swap, a perception-vs-product distinction). Don't fake it.
- When your audience is small-niche-high-value (consultants, specialists) and a soft DM CTA outperforms a high-volume comment mechanic.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Don't number a framework if the steps don't have a real order. Numbered-arbitrary-steps reads as content-marketing template.
- Don't post text-only if your audience expects design polish (e.g. B2C lifestyle audience). Form-mirrors-content has a cost when the form fights the audience's eye.
- Don't borrow "perception-vs-product" as a phrase without earning it through the body — the diagnostic only works if the framework actually delivers swaps.
