## Strategic spine

**Audience insight**: Financial advisors know AI search is changing discovery but don't know what to *do* about it — they're in the "I should look into this" phase, not the "here's my plan" phase. They'll engage with content that converts vague anxiety into a 40-minute fix.
**Big idea**: AI doesn't recommend you because your website doesn't answer the questions clients ask AI. Here's the exact prompt that shows you the gap.
**Key message**: AEO requires two unlocks — (1) FAQ content that matches buyer queries verbatim, and (2) schema markup so AI can structure-read it. Most advisors have neither.
**Product or service**: FMG Suite (the author's company — website platform for financial advisors); the post sells the platform mid-paragraph after delivering the free value.
**Channel mix**: LinkedIn organic (primary), comment-to-DM funnel → LinkedIn Live next-week (event drumbeat)

## Creative move

**What it did**: Long-form educational LinkedIn post that opens with an audit credential (136 websites), names a specific common failure (websites don't answer AI-buyer questions), hands the reader a **copy-paste prompt** to self-diagnose, introduces a secondary technical concept (schema markup) the reader hadn't thought to ask about, mentions the product as a parenthetical solve, and closes with a community CTA + a lifestyle photo (Sorrento hotel) to humanize.

**The move** (bullets):
- **Audit-credential hook**: "I just audited 136 [thing]" front-loads authority in 10 words. The number is specific and large enough to be hard-earned.
- **Curiosity-gap construction**: "might surprise you" + "Most advisor websites don't…" sets up a self-diagnosis question every reader will run on themselves.
- **Free prompt as the deliverable**: the post gives away the actual prompt to self-audit. Reader gets a 2-minute self-test before any pitch — earns trust before asking for anything.
- **Layered teach** — names problem (AEO), gives prompt (self-diagnose), names secondary unlock (schema markup), names why it matters (structure-readable to AI), drops product (FMG widget does this automatically). Each layer adds value before the product appears.
- **Mid-post parenthetical product mention**: the FMG plug is in parentheses, mid-paragraph, after the free value. Reads as a helpful aside, not a pitch turn.
- **Comment-trigger-word CTA**: "Comment 'GAP'" creates a single-word commenting affordance that's low-effort + algorithmically valuable (comments boost reach). Pairs with a specific next-step (LinkedIn Live next week).
- **Lifestyle photo tail**: the unrelated Sorrento hotel mention + photo softens the commercial weight of the post and signals "this is a person, not a brand account."

**Why it worked** (bullets):
- The audit credential is verifiable and concrete — 136 is more believable than "I've audited countless…"
- The free prompt is genuinely useful and copy-pastable — the giveaway IS the value, not a teaser for value.
- Schema markup is a real technical unlock most advisors don't know; teaching it positions the author as competent at the next level, not just the obvious one.
- The mid-post product mention is the lowest-friction sales touch possible — buyer-led discovery rather than seller-led pitch.
- Comment-mechanic generates the algorithmic signal LinkedIn rewards (108 comments in a week on a B2B-niche post is exceptional).

**When to steal**:
- LinkedIn long-form posts where you have a real audit / dataset to anchor the hook.
- Educational posts that need to sell a product without sounding like ads — the "free prompt + parenthetical product" structure is the cleanest version of "give before you ask."
- When you're driving to a community event (LinkedIn Live, webinar, AMA) and need a comment mechanic.
- Niche-B2B audiences where credibility comes from specificity (the number 136, the named technique "schema markup", the named tool "FMG widget") rather than from broad claims.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Don't open with an audit credential you don't actually have — fabricating "I audited 136" breaks the only trust signal that does the work.
- Don't drop the lifestyle-photo tail unless it reads as genuinely personal. A stock-photo "lifestyle" image undoes the humanizing move.
- Don't bury the free value — the prompt has to be copy-pastable in 2 seconds. If the reader has to hunt for it, the structure fails.
- The mid-post product mention only works if the free value is genuinely standalone. If the prompt secretly requires the product, the reader feels baited.

## Visual direction

**Hero visual description**: Native LinkedIn long-form post. No top-of-post image. Body copy follows LinkedIn's native paragraph rhythm — short single-sentence paragraphs, ample line breaks, ALL-CAPS for emphasis ("SCHEMA MARKUP"), 🔴 + 👉 + ✨ emoji used sparingly as visual anchors. The only image is a Sorrento hotel photo placed at the very end as the "lifestyle tail."

**Palette**: Native LinkedIn (white card + black text + LinkedIn blue links). The Sorrento photo carries warm Mediterranean tones (terracotta, ochre, sky-blue) — visually un-related to the body content, which is the point.

**Typography**: LinkedIn's native sans-serif body type. Hierarchy carried via paragraph breaks + emoji + ALL-CAPS — not via font weight or size (the platform doesn't permit those).

**Photography / illustration style**: Single tail photo only — vacation-grade lifestyle photography, not commissioned. Likely a personal-camera-roll shot. The amateur register IS the move; a polished image would read as a brand asset.

**Composition pattern**: Hook line → audit credential → problem statement → free prompt (set off as quoted block) → secondary concept (schema markup) → product mention (parenthetical) → CTA → personal tail. Repeating-rhythm: each section is 1–3 short paragraphs, separated by visual breath.

**Mood**: Warm-authoritative, slightly indie. Reads as a smart practitioner posting from their own desk, not a brand pushing content. The Sorrento tail reinforces "person, not brand."
