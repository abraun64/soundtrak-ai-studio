## Big idea
Loom let users record screen+webcam videos and share via link. Every recipient of a Loom video saw the watermark, the format, the playback experience — and many became users. The product was the campaign; growth was the marketing.

## Why it worked
- Every shared video was implicit product demo + brand exposure to the recipient
- Free tier removed friction for the sender; recipient experience was the trial
- Async-work cultural trend (especially post-2020) gave Loom timing advantage
- Watermark + standardised playback created brand-asset consistency across millions of user-generated videos
- Identity-reinforcement: senders became "Loom people" who used video where others wrote emails — productivity identity signal
- Educational secondary: the act of receiving and watching a Loom video taught the recipient how to use Loom

## What a CD should steal
- Product-led growth: when the product itself is shareable, the share IS the campaign — design for it from product, not marketing
- Watermark + standard format on user-generated output = compounding brand exposure at near-zero marginal cost
- Free-tier removes the sender's friction; recipient's curiosity becomes the funnel
- Time the launch to a cultural-trend window (async, remote, etc.) where audience adoption is already accelerating
- Identity-reinforcement at the productivity layer is durable — switching cost is workflow-deep, not feature-deep

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Product-not-shareable: not all products have shareable output that exposes the brand
- Watermark backlash: paid users want to remove the watermark; the free tier's exposure can erode as users upgrade
- Cultural-moment dependence: when async-work novelty fades, growth slows
- Acquisition risk: Loom's acquisition by Atlassian raised concerns about brand voice consolidation; PLG brands are vulnerable to acquirer-driven identity shifts
