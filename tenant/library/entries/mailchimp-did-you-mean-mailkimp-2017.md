## Big idea
A Serial podcast guest had mispronounced "Mailchimp" as "MailKimp" in a now-famous ad-read. Mailchimp turned that mispronunciation into a 9-execution platform: a fake fashion brand "FailChimp," a movie called "JailBlimp," a hot sauce called "KaleLimp," a band called "MaleCrimp" — all real, all available, all gloriously absurd.

## Why it worked
- Found-creative: the source material (an actual mispronunciation in the wild) gave the campaign cultural legitimacy
- Multi-execution proliferation made it a treasure hunt — discovering the next one was the participation
- Tone matched Mailchimp's existing irreverent identity — no brand-stretch credibility problem
- Worked for SMB B2B audience who appreciated the absurdism and the craft simultaneously
- Punched way above budget — distinctive enough that small spend felt huge
- The "did you mean" headline framing pre-empted SEO confusion as a creative asset

## What a CD should steal
- Found-creative: when something happens to your brand in the wild, can it become the campaign?
- Multi-execution proliferation as participation mechanic — let the audience discover, not just consume
- Absurdism in B2B works when the audience is sophisticated enough to be flattered by it
- "Did you mean" framing — turning a category friction into the creative

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Found-creative without found source: manufactured "happened to us" stories are visible from miles away
- Multi-execution requires production discipline — 9 mediocre executions kill what 3 great ones would have built
- Absurdism in regulated / corporate B2B: lands as inappropriate; know the category's permission
- Cannes-bait: campaigns optimised for awards but with thin business case — Mailchimp's worked because it actually drove SMB trial
