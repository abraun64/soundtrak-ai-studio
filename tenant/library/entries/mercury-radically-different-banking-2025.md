## Strategic spine

**Audience insight**: Founders treat business banking as a tax — slow, dumb, hostile UX they tolerate because the alternatives are worse. They will switch banks the moment one stops feeling like 1998.
**Big idea**: Banking that behaves like software, not banking that has software bolted on.
**Key message**: Open an account in 10 minutes, run every money operation in one app, and stop fighting your bank.
**Product or service**: Business checking + savings + cards + treasury + bill pay + accounting sync (full money-ops stack)
**Channel mix**: Marketing site (primary acquisition surface) · founder testimonials · press placement (Fortune / WSJ / Fast Company) · in-product

## Creative move

**What it did**: Built a marketing site that proves the product thesis through its own design — feels like software, not like a bank. Hero is a one-line claim + 10-minute promise; product screenshots and animated UI sequences do the explaining where traditional fintech sites use stock photography of professionals smiling at laptops.

**The move** (bullets):
- **Headline as posture, not feature**: "Radically different banking" stakes a worldview before any feature list.
- **Product screenshots replace stock photography**: every section anchors on an actual product surface (bill pay flow, card creation, invoicing) — the marketing IS the demo.
- **Founder testimonials over enterprise logos**: named founders of Linear / Supabase / Gainful / Ways & Means, with sharp quotes about workflow change ("everything can be done in 1–2 minutes"), not generic praise.
- **Metric-driven social proof, specific not vague**: 300K+ users / 1 in 3 startups / $20B monthly / 4.9★ — concrete numbers signal scale without consultant-speak.
- **Zero-anchors + yield-anchors as differentiators**: "$0 fees" and "3.66% yield" used as headline calls, not buried in pricing.
- **Proactive transparency move**: explicit "not a bank" disclosure + named partner banks (Choice, Column) — turns a regulatory must-disclose into a trust signal.

**Why it worked** (bullets):
- The product thesis (banking-as-software) and the marketing surface are the same thing — the medium proves the message.
- Founder testimonials are the highest-trust signal in this audience and Mercury treats them as primary, not decorative.
- The animated illustration system (geometric, abstract, branded) sidesteps both stock-photo cliché AND the "we hired an illustrator" trap — illustrations explain mechanics, not vibe.
- Numbers + named founders = no consultant-blandness; everything is specific.

**When to steal**:
- B2B SaaS marketing site where the product surface IS the most persuasive demo.
- Category where the incumbent's UX is the actual enemy (banking, accounting, HR, procurement, insurance).
- Audiences that will read a real founder testimonial but skip a "Fortune 500 customers trust us" wall.
- When the product has shipped enough that you can replace renderings with real screenshots.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Don't lift the illustrated geometric style without earning it through actual product polish — Mercury's site reads as software-y because the actual product matches; clone the visuals onto a clunky product and the gap becomes the message.
- Don't replicate "300K+ users" without numbers you'd defend in a deposition — fake or directionally-fudged metrics break trust faster than vague ones.
- Don't go "Radically different X" tagline unless you can actually point at the radical difference in 10 seconds of site scroll.

## Visual direction

**Hero visual description**: Clean white surface, minimal navigation, a single dominant headline ("Radically different banking") in large weight-medium sans, a short two-line subheadline, and a single CTA. Below the fold: alternating text/image rows where the image is a product screenshot or an animated UI sequence (e.g., card-creation steps animating in place), not stock photography.

**Palette**: Whites + warm off-whites · accent blues + teals · high-contrast text (near-black on white) · sparing use of brand greens for positive metrics. Feels closer to Stripe / Linear than to traditional bank navy + red.

**Typography**: Custom-feeling sans-serif (likely a paid typeface — visually adjacent to "Söhne" / "Inter Display" weight choices). Tight letter-spacing on headlines, generous leading on body. Hierarchy carries by weight + size, not by colour.

**Photography / illustration style**: Two visual systems running side-by-side:
1. **Geometric abstract illustrations** — overlapping circles, dot patterns, upward graph lines, stacked cards, toggle switches. Brand-coloured. Used to explain mechanics without product screenshots.
2. **Real product screenshots + lifestyle founder photography** — actual UI, actual people, no stock.

**Composition pattern**: Generous whitespace, alternating text-left / image-right rows, full-bleed coloured panels for moments of pause, mobile-first responsive grid. Quiet sections separated by visual breath, not by busy dividers.

**Mood**: Optimistic, fast-moving, software-y, slightly mischievous (the headline literally says the incumbents aren't worth your time). Professional but not corporate.
