## Why this is in the library

Cited in the operator's Netwealth IQ Report *Creating Loyalty That Lasts* as an exemplar for **Value Driver 5: Operational transparency**. The lesson: **a single annual artifact that summarises past + present + future** can replace multiple disconnected check-ins as the operational-transparency primary signal.

## The move

**What it does**: US-based firm that provides clients with a year-end review that addresses past achievements, current status, and future tasks. Three structural sections:

1. **Timeline recap** — accomplishments from the past year
2. **Colour-coded status updates** — where clients stand in each planning category (green = on-track, yellow = attention, red = action needed)
3. **Action items** — clear statement of what to do next

**The move** (bullets):
- **One annual document, three time horizons**: past (timeline recap) + present (status) + future (actions) — gives the client a complete state-of-relationship view
- **Colour-coding as cognitive shortcut**: traffic-light status reduces ambiguity → clients can scan it in 30 seconds and know if anything needs their attention
- **Action items at the bottom**: explicit "what to do next" closes the loop — no client thinks "great summary but what do I do?"
- **Executive-summary format**: pitched for skim-readability with depth available if the client wants it

**Why it works** (behavioural science):
- **Operational transparency (Norton)**: visible effort + outcomes = perceived value rises
- **Cognitive ease**: traffic-light + clear action items = low cognitive load = high comprehension
- **Goal gradient effect**: showing progress against goals keeps clients committed

**When to steal**:
- Any professional services firm with recurring engagements where the client needs to feel the relationship is "alive" between major touchpoints — accounting (annual tax review), legal (matter status update), consulting (engagement health check), agency (campaign year-in-review)
- When a single dense artifact can replace a flurry of micro-updates that nobody reads
- When clients ask "where are we at?" and you'd rather they answer that themselves from a document

**Anti-patterns**:
- Don't make the timeline a victory lap — clients can tell when the document is performance theatre vs. honest accounting
- Don't omit red/amber items to look good — colour-coding only works if it's honest. A document of all-green reads as not-credible.
- Don't bury the action items at the end without highlighting them — they're the load-bearing element
