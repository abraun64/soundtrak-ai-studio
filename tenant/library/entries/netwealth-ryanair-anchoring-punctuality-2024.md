## Why this is in the library

Cited in the operator's Netwealth IQ Report *Creating Loyalty That Lasts* as a **cross-industry** exemplar for **Value Driver 9: Prompt and timely support**. The lesson: **one repeatedly-stated metric becomes the brand anchor** — and a single dominant proof point makes other concerns less salient, even when those other concerns are legitimate (Ryanair's "but baggage fees!" critique becomes a footnote next to 93% punctuality).

## The move

**What it does**: consistently highlights on-time flight statistics (93% punctuality) to assure passengers of its reliability. By repeatedly emphasising this single metric, the brand leverages the anchoring effect — one prominent piece of information shapes overall perception.

**The move** (bullets):
- **Single metric, infinitely repeated**: 93% punctuality is The Number, mentioned at every touchpoint — boarding pass, terminal screens, in-flight announcement, website
- **Anchoring as positioning**: when one metric is dominant, other concerns (baggage fees, comfort, customer service) become less salient
- **Availability heuristic engaged**: the last thing customers remember about Ryanair is "they're on time" → that's what they associate with the brand
- **Verifiable + specific**: 93% (not "great reliability") is auditable and beats fuzzy claims

**Why it works** (behavioural science):
- **Anchoring (Kahneman/Tversky)**: a dominant first-encountered number frames all subsequent judgments about the service
- **Availability heuristic (Tversky/Kahneman)**: easily-recalled punctuality stat outweighs vaguely-recalled other complaints
- **Cognitive ease**: one number is easier to process and remember than a balanced scorecard

**When to steal**:
- Any professional services firm with one defensible operational metric clients care about — response time (<24hr), success rate (% of cases won, % of campaigns hitting target), retention rate (% of clients > 5 years), etc.
- When you can verify the metric without dispute and repeat it across every touchpoint
- When your weaknesses are legitimate but not category-killers — anchoring on the strength makes them background noise

**Anti-patterns**:
- **Don't anchor on a number you can't sustain** — if punctuality drops to 75%, the brand claim becomes the joke
- Don't anchor on a metric clients don't actually care about — "We answer emails in 4 minutes 17 seconds" is precise but useless if clients value depth over speed
- Don't anchor on multiple metrics simultaneously — the cognitive shortcut requires one dominant number, not a balanced scorecard
