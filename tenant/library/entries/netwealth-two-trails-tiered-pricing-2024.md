## Why this is in the library

Cited in the operator's Netwealth IQ Report *Creating Loyalty That Lasts* as an exemplar for **Value Driver 5: Operational transparency**. The lesson: **named, tiered pricing packages + a visible journey map** convert the "what does it cost? what do I get?" anxiety into a clean shop-by-tier decision.

## The move

**What it does**: takes a clear and straightforward approach to explaining both its process and fees. Clients choose between two distinct service tiers — **"Summit Wealth"** and **"Base Camp"**. Each tier openly details what it provides and how much it costs.

Tier names are not "Premium / Basic" — they're metaphor-led (Summit = peak goals; Base Camp = foundational setup). The website outlines each stage of the journey from creating a map to regular check-ins.

**The move** (bullets):
- **Named tiers with metaphor**: "Summit Wealth" vs. "Base Camp" anchors the decision in journey language, not enterprise-software language ("Premium" / "Standard" / "Enterprise")
- **Tiered pricing clarity**: clear service levels reduce ambiguity about costs and scope → clients feel in control of spending
- **Step-by-step process visualisation**: the website outlines each stage of the engagement journey → reduces uncertainty aversion + boosts confidence in firm's expertise
- **Self-service-by-tier**: clients self-select into the right level, reducing sales-conversation friction

**Why it works** (behavioural science):
- **Operational transparency**: behind-the-scenes process + pricing visible = perceived value rises
- **Reducing uncertainty aversion**: pre-engagement, the journey map removes the "what happens after I sign?" anxiety
- **Cognitive ease**: two tiers (not six) makes the decision tractable

**When to steal**:
- Any professional services firm where prospects struggle to evaluate "which level is right for me?" — legal, accounting, consulting, coaching, agency
- When you can productise into 2-3 named tiers (more than 3 reintroduces choice paralysis)
- When you can show the journey on a single landing page — both tiers visible, the journey-map shared

**Anti-patterns**:
- Don't name tiers after corporate hierarchy ("Enterprise / Pro / Basic") — feels like enterprise SaaS, not professional services
- Don't make Tier 1 useless — it should be a legitimate entry, not a dummy to make Tier 2 look better
- Don't have more than 3 tiers — choice paralysis kills the operational-transparency benefit
- Don't hide the step-by-step process behind a "Contact us" form — the visibility IS the move
