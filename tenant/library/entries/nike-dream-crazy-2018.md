## Big idea
Nike used Colin Kaepernick — the NFL quarterback who became a lightning rod for kneeling during the national anthem — as the face of its 30th-anniversary Just Do It campaign. The hero film was a manifesto for athletes whose pursuit of greatness was inseparable from social courage.

## Why it worked
- Took a *real* position on a polarising issue when most brands hedged — earned permission to be in the conversation
- The audience (younger, urban, diverse) had been waiting for a major brand to back the player they backed
- Boycott imagery (people burning Nike shoes) became free additional media; the people burning shoes weren't Nike's target audience anyway
- Hero film + supporting echo (OOH portraits of other "crazy dreamer" athletes) carried for months

## What a CD should steal
- The clarity of a real stake — you can't be famous for everything; pick the position your audience needs you to take
- The discipline of audience selection — Nike *chose* who it was for, knowing that meant choosing who it wasn't for
- Counter-programming as creative direction: when every competitor zigs, the brand with permission can zag
- Hero asset designed to *generate* the controversy that funds the campaign

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Brand-without-permission: take a Kaepernick-scale stance without history backing it and the audience smells the opportunism (Pepsi/Kendall Jenner)
- Half-stake hedging: try to please both sides — alienates both
- Walk-mismatch: take a stance the rest of the company doesn't reflect — the credibility burns first
- Borrowed courage: backing a cause for visibility without willingness to absorb the backlash
