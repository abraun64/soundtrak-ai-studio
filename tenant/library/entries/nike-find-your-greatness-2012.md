## Big idea
Adidas was the official sponsor of the 2012 London Olympics. Nike, locked out of official sponsorship, ran "Find Your Greatness" — a hero film of everyday people (including a now-iconic chubby kid jogging) finding their own greatness in their own lives. The campaign celebrated trying, not winning.

## Why it worked
- Counter-programmed the Olympics' elite-only framing with everyday inclusivity — broader emotional resonance
- Ambush-marketing tactic worked because the brand's stance (greatness for everyone) genuinely contradicted the official sponsor's elitism
- Real, unidealised people in the film — the chubby-kid-jogging shot became iconic precisely because nobody had cast a chubby kid in a Nike ad before
- Brand's broader Just Do It platform absorbed and amplified the new execution
- Generated more conversation than the official sponsor's Olympics ads despite spending a fraction

## What a CD should steal
- Ambush-marketing works when the brand's position genuinely conflicts with the incumbent's — not as a stunt, as a stance
- Real people, including unidealised real people, can break category convention in a category obsessed with athletic perfection
- Counter-program the dominant cultural framing of a moment — what is everyone else celebrating, and what's the opposite truth?
- Slot new executions into an established platform to compound equity rather than restart it

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Ambush-without-position: doing the gesture without the underlying stance reads as cheap
- Real-people-as-tokenism: cast for "diversity" without specificity and it falls flat
- Olympic-style moments are rare: not every category has a clear elitism to counter
