## Big idea
Notion empowered a creator economy around itself. YouTube and TikTok creators built Notion templates (productivity systems, journals, project trackers), monetised them, and produced thousands of hours of free content teaching the audience how to use Notion. The product's flexibility became the campaign's surface area.

## Why it worked
- Product flexibility made template creation legitimately useful — not a marketing gimmick, real value exchange
- Creators with audiences pre-built distribution Notion didn't have to fund
- Template marketplace gave creators income — economic alignment kept them producing
- Identity-reinforcement effect: "I'm a Notion person" became a productivity-identity signal in tech-adjacent audiences
- Educational pipeline secondary: thousands of YouTube tutorials onboarded users for free, replacing what would otherwise have been a customer-education content team
- Compounding flywheel: more users → more creators → more templates → more reasons to adopt

## What a CD should steal
- If your product is genuinely flexible/extensible, the creator economy is your distribution
- Economic alignment with creators (template marketplace, ambassador programmes) keeps the flywheel turning
- Free user-generated education replaces internal content investment — and is more credible
- Identity-reinforcement is the moat; switching costs are emotional, not just functional
- Low paid-media floor: with strong creator economics, paid marketing becomes optional, not core

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Forced creator programmes: pay creators to make content without genuine product fit — feels astroturfed
- Product not flexible enough to template: many SaaS products can't run this play
- Creator dependence: if creator economics shift (platform algorithms, monetisation rules), the flywheel slows
- Identity fragility: prosumer identities ("Notion person," "Roam person," "Obsidian person") are notoriously switchable as new tools emerge
