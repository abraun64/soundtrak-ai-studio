## Big idea
Wieden+Kennedy + Old Spice spotted that women bought 60%+ of men's bodywash and made the spot for them. Then, the campaign's "real" turn: a 48-hour real-time response push where actor Isaiah Mustafa filmed 186 personalised video replies to tweets, comments, and celebrity mentions — turning the ad into a conversation.

## Why it worked
- Reframed the buyer (women) without abandoning the user (men) — sophisticated audience insight
- Tonal swing — absurdist confidence, surrealist sets, fourth-wall — gave a tired brand instant cultural permission
- The real-time response phase was the participation layer; the audience didn't just watch, they got *replied to*
- Wildly quotable lines and visual gags engineered for sharing
- Production discipline: response videos were made in 24-48 hours, on the same set, in the same tone

## What a CD should steal
- Audience-reframe: who's actually buying, vs. who's using? The buyer often isn't the user
- Tonal absurdism as differentiation — works in tired categories where every competitor's tone is identical
- Real-time response as a second act — what's the participation surface after the hero asset lands?
- Production-set-as-platform: a stage that can be re-used at speed for as long as the cultural conversation lasts

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Absurdism without a real insight underneath — comedy without strategy is just noise
- Real-time response without the talent/infrastructure to sustain it — flames out in 24h, embarrassing
- Cargo-cult: brands forcing absurdist comedy because Old Spice did, regardless of category permission
- One-and-done: Old Spice itself struggled to follow up; the platform was harder to extend than to launch
