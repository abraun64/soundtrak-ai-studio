## Big idea
On Black Friday 2011, Patagonia ran a full-page New York Times ad with a photo of its best-selling R2 jacket and the headline "Don't Buy This Jacket." Body copy explained the environmental cost of producing the jacket and asked readers to consume less.

## Why it worked
- The paradox earned attention no straight ad could buy
- Walked the talk — Patagonia's Worn Wear repair programme, 1% for the Planet commitment, and B Corp status backed the stance with operational proof
- Increased sales counter-intuitively: customers rewarded brands that genuinely walk their stated values
- Identity-reinforcement secondary effect: existing Patagonia customers felt validated in their identity; new customers were recruited by the courage of the move
- Multi-year platform: Patagonia has continued this stance (suing the Trump administration, "this company is now a trust for the planet," etc.)

## What a CD should steal
- Paradox as headline architecture — "don't do X" framing instantly arrests scroll
- Sacrifice-as-credibility — the brand that demonstrates willingness to lose business for its stance earns the trust to keep it
- Operational backing matters more than the ad — the ad is the visible 5%; the other 95% is what the company actually does
- Long-game thinking: this ad keeps paying dividends 15 years later

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Sacrifice-theatre: brand makes the gesture, then walks it back operationally — accelerates cynicism
- "Don't buy this" without the operations: a fashion brand running this ad would be eviscerated
- Single-stance brands: a brand without a multi-decade values track record claiming this position reads as opportunistic
- Replicability trap: any imitator looks derivative; this is a one-time move for any given brand
