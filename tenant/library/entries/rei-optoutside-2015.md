## Big idea
On Black Friday 2015 — retail's biggest day — REI closed all 143 stores, gave 12,000 employees a paid day off to be outdoors, and refused online orders for the day. The hashtag #OptOutside invited customers to do the same.

## Why it worked
- Sacrifice-as-credibility — the operational cost of closing on Black Friday was real and visible; the gesture had skin in the game
- Co-op identity match — REI is structurally a member-owned co-op; the action reinforced what the brand structurally is
- Counter-category positioning — every retailer screaming sale, REI alone closed; impossible to ignore
- Employee gesture: paid leave for staff was internal-brand AND external-brand at once
- Multi-year platform — repeats every Black Friday; other retailers and even some governments have joined over time
- Created a positive cultural ritual rather than just a media moment

## What a CD should steal
- Sacrifice-as-credibility: visible operational cost is the proof; ad spend is not
- Co-op / employee-led gestures double the audience — internal and external simultaneously
- Counter-category timing: when every competitor is doing X, the brand that does ¬X (and means it) owns a moment
- Build a repeating ritual, not a one-off — annual moments compound brand equity
- Earned-media calculus: lost sales were measured; earned media + brand-equity lift outweighed by a wide margin

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Sacrifice-theatre: pretend to close, run online sales quietly — REI's credibility came from being absolute
- Co-op-without-structure: only REI could do this because it's literally a co-op; a public retailer answering to shareholders couldn't
- Multi-year fatigue: by year 5, the gesture is expected, not surprising; needs evolution
- Cause-without-fit: brands without genuine outdoor / sustainability authority attempting similar gestures look opportunistic
