## Big idea
Slack avoided the trap of positioning as "team chat" (a category dominated by HipChat and Microsoft Teams) and instead positioned the product as the place where work itself happens — replacing email as the workplace's connective tissue. Marketing centred on customer-story films showing how Slack changed the texture of a workday, not feature comparisons.

## Why it worked
- Reframed the category — "team chat" is replaceable; "where work happens" isn't
- Customer-story films focused on the *texture* of the workday change, not the feature set — emotional resonance over feature-pitching
- Identity-reinforcement secondary effect: "we're a Slack company" became professional shorthand for a workplace culture
- Founder Stewart Butterfield's writing (the famous "We Don't Sell Saddles Here" memo, made public) gave the brand a voice
- Used product-led growth + brand marketing together — the brand made the product easier to adopt; the product made the brand more credible

## What a CD should steal
- Reframe the category above the obvious one — don't compete in a category you can be commoditised in
- Customer-story films that focus on the *change* in a day, not the features — emotional resonance + practical proof
- Product-led growth and brand marketing as paired motions, not competing ones
- Founder-as-essayist — Butterfield's longer-form public writing built brand credibility no ad could buy

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Reframe-without-product-fit: claim a category your product can't actually own
- Customer-story-as-template: scripted, on-brand customer stories read fake; the rough-edge specificity is what makes them work
- Founder-voice fade: when Butterfield left post-Salesforce-acquisition, Slack's distinctive voice diminished noticeably
