## Strategic spine
**Audience**: B2B leaders, founders, CMOs who already research their direct customers but stay stuck as a vendor. **Big idea**: most B2B research points at one relationship (you ↔ your customer) and has a ceiling — it only surfaces what the customer already knows about themselves; researching the customer's *customer* breaks that ceiling. **Key message**: the research that shifts you from vendor to strategic partner is about your customer's market, not your product. **Proof**: from 2017 Netwealth surveyed 1,000+ investors annually (the people advisers served), which reframed product messaging *and* roadmap (mobile-first); plus Shopify's BFCM consumer data for merchants and Salesforce's State of the Connected Customer.

## Creative move
**What it did**: A ~6-minute master-brand article that takes a familiar B2B habit (research your customers) and pushes it one node downstream (research your customers' customers), with a concrete recruiter scene, the Netwealth proof, two external examples, and a low-cost on-ramp for the resource objection.

**The move** (portable tactic + the master long-form structure):
- **Concrete-scene cold open**: the recruiter who tracked her client's metrics until research revealed the *interviewers* valued being told early when a brief was unrealistic.
- **Name what's accurate but incomplete**: direct-customer research is "accurate and worth having, but it has a ceiling" — concede the standard practice before extending it.
- **The reframe in one line**: "The research that changes how customers see you isn't about your product. It's about their market."
- **Proof with real numbers**: Netwealth's 1,000+ investor survey reframed the client portal and managed accounts *and* redirected product to mobile-first; Shopify's 76M-consumer BFCM data; Salesforce's 16,000-consumer survey.
- **"If you apply one thing from this 🛠️"** → executable list: research downstream / upstream / adjacent; share insights back in usable form; name the positioning explicitly; use it in sales and in product.
- **The resource question (fair objection)**: a 1,000-person survey is a serious investment; the on-ramp is "ten structured conversations with your customer's clients" — principle first, scale follows the evidence.
- **No-recap close**: "Vendors know their product. Strategic partners know the market their customers are trying to win."

**Why it worked**:
- The downstream move is a genuinely fresh tactic for B2B teams who think research = NPS + win/loss.
- Conceding the ceiling of standard research earns the extension — thinking-partner register.
- The "ten conversations" on-ramp defuses the budget objection and creates a soft CTA (the gift download).

**When to steal**:
- Any B2B/pro-services tenant wanting to move from vendor to indispensable partner — commission research on the customer's customer.
- When the brief is "differentiate in a same-feature category" — downstream intelligence is the differentiator.
- For the master long-form voice: concrete open, concede-then-extend, fair-objection on-ramp, no-recap close.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Researching downstream without sharing it back "in usable form" — a filed white paper changes nothing; it must travel into the customer's own commercial conversations.
- Over-scoping the first effort; the article's own warning is to start at ten conversations, not a 1,000-person panel.
- Confusing this with adjacent-market research for your own benefit — the intelligence has to serve the customer's relationship with *their* market.
