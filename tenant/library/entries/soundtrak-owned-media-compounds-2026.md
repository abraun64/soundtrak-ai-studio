## Strategic spine
**Audience**: CMOs, founders, PE-backed CEOs deciding the brand-vs-performance budget split. **Big idea**: performance media is a rental — it goes to zero the moment you stop paying; owned media (newsletter, podcast, research programme, content library) is an asset that compounds. **Key message**: the first edition costs what the fiftieth does, but by the fiftieth you own an audience, a ranking body of work, and a brand presence independent of media budget. **Proof**: Netwealth (ASX:NWL) built owned media — research reports, CEO/industry podcasts, sustained thought leadership — to create mental availability, so advisers had Netwealth on the shortlist before any RFP. "The brand didn't win the deal. It made sure we were on the shortlist."

## Creative move
**What it did**: A ~5-minute master-brand article reframing the brand-vs-performance debate as appreciate-vs-depreciate, with compounding mechanics, an allocation heuristic, the Netwealth proof, common-mistake warnings, and the theory anchors (Byron Sharp, 95-5, Seth Godin).

**The move** (portable framework + master long-form structure):
- **Flat-claim open**: "Every dollar spent on paid media produces results exactly proportional to what you spend, and stops the moment you stop." States the position immediately, then concedes performance has a real function — fair, not dismissive.
- **The one-line reframe** (pull-quote): "The difference between a campaign and an asset is what happens when you stop paying. One goes to zero. One keeps going."
- **Mechanics as a list**: each newsletter edition grows the base; each report earns persistent citations/backlinks; podcast transcripts compound SEO; owned audiences are direct access without a platform tax.
- **Allocation heuristic**: one dollar → brand; hundred dollars → over-index brand, mix by in-market %; the gumball-machine analogy; "if <30% of your audience is in-market, prioritise brand and owned."
- **B2B-specific truth**: in-market B2B buyers don't Google category terms — they go straight to brands they already know; if you're not in their head first, you're not on the shortlist.
- **Theory anchors named**: Byron Sharp's mental availability, the 95-5 rule, Seth Godin's permission marketing — credibility scaffolding.
- **No-recap close** built into "confusing being seen with being remembered."

**Why it worked**:
- The appreciate-vs-depreciate frame is sticky and survives the budget meeting.
- Conceding performance's real function keeps it credible, not ideological.
- The Netwealth shortlist line is the proof that converts the theory into a commercial outcome.

**When to steal**:
- Any tenant over-indexed on performance and asking why pipeline stalls when spend pauses.
- B2B/considered-purchase categories where the buying window is rare and mental availability decides the shortlist.
- For the master long-form voice: flat-claim open, concede-the-counter, one-line reframe, named theory anchors, no-recap close.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- Treating owned media as a campaign — the compounding only appears with sustained, multi-year commitment.
- Over-applying search/performance logic to B2B, where buyers go direct to known vendors.
- Confusing reach with mental availability — impressions on someone who won't remember you served no purpose.
