## Strategic spine
**Audience**: CMOs, founders, marketing leaders facing AI-driven content commoditisation. **Big idea**: when everyone can produce unlimited content at near-zero cost, content stops being an advantage — the only defensible content is proprietary research a competitor can't replicate. **Key message**: don't ask how to make more; ask what you can publish that no one else can. **Proof**: Netwealth's 8-year AdviceTech research (300+ firms/yr) became impossible to replicate and an industry standard; plus Michelin, Edelman Trust Barometer, Knight Frank Wealth Report, PwC CEO Survey, Spotify Wrapped — "different industries, one move."

## Creative move
**What it did**: A ~6-minute master-brand POV article arguing that AI's gift (volume) is the trap, and proprietary research is the only content that compounds. Opens on a real scene (a team with 10× the output and flat pipeline), lands the thesis, stacks cross-industry proof, gives an executable checklist, then pre-empts the obvious objection.

**The move** (the portable master-brand long-form structure):
- **Concrete-scene cold open**, not a thesis announcement: "A marketing team I spoke with recently had ten times the output… Then someone asked about pipeline."
- **Cited, specific evidence** woven into the argument: 74.2% of new web pages AI-generated (Ahrefs); 64% higher conversion for original research (TopRank/Ascend2). Numbers, not adjectives.
- **The proof stack**: one move shown across Michelin / Edelman / Knight Frank / PwC / Spotify — proves the principle is industry-agnostic.
- **"If you apply one thing from this"** → an executable list (pick a customer-relevant gap; unique-and-relevant over big; new deep-dive angle each year; build a benchmark; atomise every edition; commit annually).
- **"The fair objection"** section: names the reader's pushback ("we are not Netwealth") and holds the position honestly ("The first edition will not be a moat. The fifth will.").
- **Close on the sharpest version**, not a recap: "Dig the moat."

**Why it worked**:
- The cold open dramatises the problem before stating it — earns the thesis.
- Naming the objection and answering it honestly is the thinking-partner register; it disarms the budget-meeting rebuttal.
- Cross-industry proof makes the move portable to any tenant, not just wealth.
- One asset = many assets is stated inside the argument (white paper → keynote → buyer's guide → articles → video), which previews the atomisation play.

**When to steal**:
- Any tenant in a category being flooded by AI content — the moat is proprietary research.
- Any long-form needing the master-brand voice: concrete open, cited evidence, fair-objection section, no-recap close.
- When the brief asks for thought leadership that doubles as proof-of-method (the research moat IS the credential).

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- "Data-as-content without data" — thin or fabricated "we analysed X" framings get smelled out.
- One-off research is not a moat; the compounding only works as a committed annual programme.
- Picking a gap that flatters the product rather than serving the customer — the article warns against exactly this.

## Voice reference
**The master-brand long-form voice floor.** Producer should read this for any Soundtrak long-form: concrete scene opener, contrast over assertion, cited evidence embedded in the argument, the "fair objection" honesty move, near-zero em-dashes, and a no-recap close.
