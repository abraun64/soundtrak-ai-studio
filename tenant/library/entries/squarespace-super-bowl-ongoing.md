## Big idea
Squarespace has run consistently distinctive Super Bowl spots since 2015 — Jeff Bridges meditating on "Sleeping Tapes," Keanu Reeves riding a motorcycle, Adam Driver multiplied, Zendaya as a beach-stand entrepreneur, Adam Driver again. The format is consistent: a single celebrity, a creative premise, the URL at the end. The website-building category became Squarespace by repetition.

## Why it worked
- Repeating architecture creates cumulative brand equity — viewers know what to expect
- Celebrity choice signals taste (Bridges, Driver, Reeves are auteur-actor choices, not generic stars) — brand inherits the actor's taste
- Single URL CTA — DR discipline inside a brand-moment format
- Super Bowl moment converted to category-leader assumption: "the brand that does Super Bowl ads must be the leader"
- Behind-the-scenes content extended each Super Bowl spot for weeks
- Cumulative compounding: by year 5, Squarespace owned a Super Bowl slot in the category

## What a CD should steal
- Repeating architecture across years compounds brand equity in a way one-offs don't
- Celebrity-taste-transfer: pick actors/figures whose taste flatters the brand, not whose fame is highest
- DR discipline inside brand moments — the URL ending keeps the Super Bowl ad accountable
- Pre-empt category leadership by acting like the leader (Super Bowl ads are leader behaviour) — even before the share confirms it
- Behind-the-scenes content as a multiplier on the hero asset

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Celebrity-as-cargo: hire a star with no creative premise — feels expensive and empty
- Super Bowl misfire: a single bad Super Bowl spot in a multi-year platform can undo years of equity
- Repeating-architecture-fatigue: when the audience knows the format too well, surprise dies; needs evolution
- Cost dependence: Super Bowl spots are not feasible for most brands; the principle (repeating architecture, celebrity taste-transfer, DR discipline) is portable, the budget isn't
