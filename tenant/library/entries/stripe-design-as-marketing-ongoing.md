## Big idea
Stripe never ran a traditional brand campaign for its first decade. Instead, every developer touchpoint — the API documentation, the marketing site, the press page, the recruitment site, the annual letter, the conference (Stripe Sessions) — was crafted to a standard so visibly higher than competitors that the product earned its reputation through its surfaces.

## Why it worked
- Audience (developers) is allergic to traditional B2B advertising — recognises and rejects it on contact
- Craft signals technical seriousness — if Stripe's marketing site is this thoughtful, the API probably is too
- Every touchpoint was a recruitment tool for the next layer of the funnel — devs → CTOs → CEOs
- Sustained over 10+ years — the consistency *is* the brand; no campaign would have been more credible than the cumulative craft
- Made design itself the moat — competitors who tried to match Stripe's craft realised it required org-wide design investment, not a vendor

## What a CD should steal
- For developer / technical-buyer audiences: craft > campaign
- Every owned surface is the brand — there is no "campaign" channel separate from the product
- Sustained craft compounds in a way bursts of campaign spend never do
- Make design a sales asset — investor decks, recruitment, partner enablement all benefit
- Anti-marketing positioning is itself marketing for audiences that distrust traditional marketing

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Craft-as-veneer: pretty site, mediocre product — devs find out fast
- Cost ceiling: Stripe's design org is expensive; smaller competitors trying to match it bleed cash
- No-campaign trap: when "we don't do campaigns" becomes the policy even when a brand moment is needed, the brand misses inflection points
- Audience-dependent: works for technical/sophisticated buyers; fails for audiences that *expect* traditional brand signals
