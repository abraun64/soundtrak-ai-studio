## The style — what it is

Hand-drawn pen-and-ink line work with light watercolor washes. Editorial-cartoon vocabulary (the Steve Bell / Kneebone / Australian-newspaper-opinion-page register). Single-scene compositions where the visual joke / argument lands in one frame without requiring sequential reading.

Distinct from:
- **AI-generated illustration** (Midjourney / SDXL): even when "watercolor style" is prompted, AI versions lack the confident-line restraint and white-space discipline. They feel filled-in; this style feels gestured.
- **Slick commercial illustration** (Mailchimp Freddie-era, Slack illustrations, Stripe's geometric system): polished vector / digital painting, brand-coloured, optimised for tech-product surfaces. This style is the opposite — handmade, low-saturation, deliberately editorial.
- **Children's-book watercolor** (Quentin Blake / Oliver Jeffers): adjacent register but Kneebone-style carries political-editorial gravity Blake/Jeffers don't.

## Creative move (style-as-move)

**The move** (bullets):
- **Confident pen-line over watercolor** — line work does the meaning; watercolor adds warmth and atmosphere. Inversion of the children's-illustration default (where watercolor leads).
- **White space at the edges** — vignette effect achieved by letting the wash fade to nothing at the frame edge. Image breathes on LinkedIn's white card / opinion-page background. No hard frame.
- **Single-scene, edge-to-edge composition** — protagonist centered (the sprayer figure here), supporting characters arranged in middle and far ground (picnicking families, playground), action reads in 2 seconds. No panel sequence required.
- **Hand-lettered diegetic labels** — the hazmat tank's hand-lettered "GLYPHOSATE / PFAS / WHATEVER..." with hazard-triangle icon. The lettering is INSIDE the world, not overlaid on top. Cartoon-traditional.
- **Loose ink hatching for shadow + texture** — gives volume and movement without rendering. Reads as "drawn in 20 minutes by someone confident," not "rendered in 8 hours by someone careful."
- **Signature in the corner** — hand-lettered illustrator name. Marks the work as a person's, not a brand's.
- **Restrained palette** — washes of pale blue (sky), pale green (grass), warm earth tones (skin / clothing), occasional saturated accent (the hazard triangle, the ball on the ground). Most of the image is line on near-white.

**Why it works**:
- The hand draws trust. AI-slop fatigue is real, and audiences can clock generated illustration in 100ms. Hand-drawn-with-watercolor reads as commissioned human work even when the topic is corporate-promoted.
- Single-scene = LinkedIn-feed-friendly. The reader processes the joke without scrolling, without zooming, without expanding.
- Editorial-cartoon vocabulary borrows the gravitas of opinion-page cartooning history (Bell, Tomorrow, Oliphant). Carries "I am making an argument" energy even when the caption is gentle.
- Restraint is the differentiator in 2025's image-saturated feed — most LinkedIn images are screenshots, charts, or AI-generated stock. A real watercolor cartoon stops the scroll.

**When to steal**:
- Advocacy / educational / opinion content where the goal is emotional landing without preachiness.
- Topics where a hard truth needs a soft entry (environmental, health, policy, ethics).
- B2B founders / consultants whose brand is built on "I think for myself" — illustrated opinion-pieces signal that more than text-only posts do.
- Newsletter cover art, opinion-essay heroes, deck section-dividers, conference-talk slide backgrounds.
- When the brief explicitly rules out AI-generated illustration.

**Anti-patterns / risks**:
- **Don't pair with sales-y CTAs** ("Buy now," "Book a demo"). The style codes as editorial; the CTA betrays the editorial promise.
- **Don't AI-generate it**. Midjourney/SDXL "watercolor editorial cartoon" prompts produce uncanny-valley versions — line confidence is the tell. The integrity is in the hand. Hire Kneebone or a real illustrator. Pay for it.
- **Don't use for celebratory content**. The visual register carries critique by default; using it for "launching our new feature!" reads as ironic / self-defeating.
- **Don't over-render** — slicker is worse. If the illustrator polishes too far, the editorial energy dies.

## Visual direction (the full style spec)

**Hero visual description**: Pen-and-ink line drawing with watercolor wash over the top. Single scene composition — central figure in white hazmat suit holding a long spray hose, scene fills the frame edge to edge, action centered. Tank on figure's chest carries hand-lettered hazard label. Background characters (parents on picnic blanket, children playing on swings) arranged in middle and far ground. Sky-blue wash for upper third, pale-green wash for lower two-thirds, edges fading to white.

**Palette**:
- Sky: pale cerulean wash (Winsor & Newton "cerulean blue" register)
- Ground: pale yellow-green wash
- Skin tones: warm peach / sienna
- Clothing: muted blues, terracotta, white
- Accents (use sparingly): bright yellow (hazard triangle), red-brown (the ball)
- Lines: warm black-brown ink (not pure black — closer to sepia)
- Overall saturation low. Most of the image is line + white.

**Typography**:
- Diegetic hand-lettering on tank labels, signs, in-world text — irregular caps, ink-pen weight, slightly wobbly
- Signature: hand-lettered illustrator name bottom-left or bottom-right corner
- No overlaid type — captions live OUTSIDE the image (in the LinkedIn post body, in the article column)

**Photography / illustration style**: **Hand-drawn pen-and-ink + watercolor wash + restrained palette + signature**. Editorial-cartoon-page register. Loose confident lines. Visible underdrawing acceptable. Watercolor allowed to pool, bleed, fade to white at edges. Texture from paper tooth visible if reproduction allows.

**Composition pattern**: Single-scene, protagonist-centered, supporting cast in middle/far ground, edges-fade-to-white vignette. Sky-wash above horizon, ground-wash below, no hard horizon line. Diagonal action lines if the scene needs movement.

**Mood**: Warm despite hard topic. Gentle even when calling something out. Editorial gravity + human softness simultaneously. The contrast — cute children + sinister sprayer — IS the move; the style holds both registers at once without forcing either.
