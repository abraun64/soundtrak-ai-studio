## Big idea
Warby Parker's central category problem was that people don't buy prescription glasses online because they can't try them on. The Home Try-On programme — pick 5 frames, ship them free, try at home, send back what you don't want — solved the problem AND generated the marketing simultaneously, as customers posted try-on photos on social for friends' votes.

## Why it worked
- Solved the single biggest objection in the category — friction-removal as competitive advantage
- The mechanic generated UGC by design: deciding between 5 frames means asking friends, which means posting → social distribution at zero cost
- Pricing transparency ($95 frames including lenses) plus the programme made comparison shopping unnecessary
- Programme is also the product experience preview — try-on builds purchase confidence the in-store equivalent doesn't (you have time at home)
- Compounding identity: "I'm a Warby person" became a recognisable style choice for educated urban professionals

## What a CD should steal
- The mechanic-that-removes-objection-AND-generates-UGC double — design for both at once, not separately
- Programme as marketing — the operational commitment IS the message
- Friend-vote dynamic — when buying decisions require third-party input, the marketing surface multiplies
- Identity-tier-by-product — the frame choice becomes self-expression, which compounds in user reviews and social posts

## Anti-patterns / risks
- Programme without operational backing — if free shipping/returns aren't fully funded, the offer collapses
- UGC dependence — if the audience stops posting, the marketing engine stops
- Easily copied: the mechanic was widely copied by competitors (Zenni, Eyebuydirect), eroding Warby's differentiation; original advantage was first-mover + brand craft
- Margin pressure: the programme is cost-heavy and only works at scale
