# 📼 Retro / nostalgic

Borrows a specific era's visual language — 70s earth tones, 80s neon + chrome, 90s grunge-halftone, film grain, VHS/cassette texture, vintage packaging — to trigger warmth and familiarity. **Nostalgia lowers the guard**: the audience feels affection before they evaluate. Works only when the era chosen actually means something to the audience.

## When to pick this for §4 Visual architecture

- Brands leaning on heritage, or challengers borrowing the warmth of a beloved era
- CPG / food / drink / entertainment where affection drives choice
- Aperture or Attention / fame plays that want instant emotional shorthand
- Audiences with a shared cultural memory to tap (generation-specific)

## Visual hallmarks

- Pick ONE era and commit — period-accurate type + colour, not a pastiche of several
- Analogue textures: film grain, halftone, print misregistration, tape artefacts, worn edges
- Imagery: vintage photography, retro illustration, period product shots
- Deliberate "imperfection" — the warmth lives in the analogue flaws
- Copy register: playful, familiar, era-appropriate

## What a CD writing §4 for this register would say

> Mood: warm, familiar, affectionate — nostalgia before evaluation. Pick ONE era and commit fully (70s earth tones / 80s neon + chrome / 90s grunge-halftone). Textures: film grain, halftone, print misregistration, analogue wear — the imperfection is the warmth. Type + palette period-accurate. Imagery: vintage or retro-illustrated. Copy: playful, era-appropriate. No-go: mixing eras (reads as costume), clean-digital polish that kills the analogue warmth, nostalgia for an era the audience never lived.

## Anti-patterns

- **Costume, not homage**: era cues mixed or half-committed — reads as a theme party.
- **Empty nostalgia**: borrowed warmth with no link to the brand or product.
- **Wrong-era**: a period the target audience has no memory of or affection for.
- **Polish kills it**: over-cleaning the analogue textures removes the exact thing that works.
