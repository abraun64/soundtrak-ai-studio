# The AI-Authorship Effect — why AI-written marketing triggers moral disgust

**Vertical**: universal · **Layer**: human-behaviour · **Audience**: all (buyers · marketing leaders · founders) · **Topic**: AI-generated content / authenticity / taste premium
**Added by**: Phase-1 insight scan — Soundtrak Campaign 1 (ai-system-overview / book-5-calls), 2026-06-22.
**Segment relevance**: the load-bearing human-layer reframe under Soundtrak's whole positioning — explains *why generic-AI marketing offends* (Seg 1 insight 1.2) and why the scale-up leader's "slop" dread is a real, evidenced risk, not vanity (Seg 2 insight 2.1).

## Sources / citation
- **Kirk, Colleen P. & Givi, Julian** — *The AI-authorship effect: Understanding authenticity, moral disgust, and consumer responses to AI-generated marketing communications*, **Journal of Business Research** (vol. 186, 2025; published online 2024). https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0148296324004880
- Corroboration: **Deloitte Connected Consumer Survey 2024** — ~70% concerned AI-generated content will be used to deceive them; **Nuremberg Institute for Market Decisions 2025** — labelling an ad "AI-generated" lowers perceived naturalness, usefulness and purchase intent.
- Discourse corroboration: the "authenticity premium" / "human-made premium" reporting (KO Insights; Digiday "AI slop" coverage 2025–26).

## Faithful summary — key findings
- **Seven preregistered experiments**: when consumers believe an emotional marketing communication was written by AI (vs a human), positive word-of-mouth and customer loyalty drop — **even when the content is otherwise identical**.
- The mechanism is **serially mediated by perceived authenticity → moral disgust**. AI authorship reads as *inauthentic*, which provokes a quasi-moral revulsion, which suppresses engagement, loyalty and purchase intent. It is a felt, visceral response, not a quality judgment.
- **Moderators (where the effect weakens or flips)**: it attenuates for *factual* (vs emotional) messages; when AI only *edits* rather than authors; when the AI is openly disclosed as signer; and — crucially — **when consumers already assume most marketing is AI-written** (the effect normalises away as slop becomes the baseline expectation).
- Deloitte / Nuremberg corroborate the disclosure penalty: merely *knowing* content is AI-made lowers attitudes and willingness to act.

## Why it matters (for any tenant; first used for Soundtrak)
This is the empirical floor under the entire "generic AI gives generic marketing" argument. The offence a discerning buyer/founder feels at generic-AI output isn't snobbery — it's a **documented, near-universal authenticity-then-disgust response** to emotional communication that reads as machine-authored. Two consequences travel with it: (1) it validates the *human-in-the-loop, practitioner-authored* model as risk-mitigation, not just taste; (2) the "normalises away when everyone assumes AI" moderator is the **commoditisation trap** — once the market expects slop, the only defensible position is *visibly* human-judged craft. Universal: applies to any tenant selling craft, taste, or human-authored work into an audience that can tell the difference (or whose customers can).
