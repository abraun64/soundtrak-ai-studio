# Charter teacher supply — the sector-wide data gap (US)

**Publisher**: Center on Reinventing Public Education (CRPE)
**Dates**: *Working Smarter, Not Harder* (analysis of 18 high-choice cities, **2017–2018**); corroborated by *A Dry Pipeline: CMO Teacher Hiring in an Uncertain Teacher Labor Market* (Taylor & Francis, **2024**).
**Added by**: Phase-1 insight scan (ACME CO Global Education Workforce Report), 2026-06-22.
**Segment relevance**: conglomerate buying groups (US charter networks / CMOs) — primary.

## Faithful summary — key data points
- In CRPE's **2017–2018 analysis of 18 high-choice cities, NO city had concrete data about teacher vacancies or retention rates in its charter schools.** Individual schools held their own hiring data, but the absence of citywide/sector visibility prevented anyone from distinguishing localized vs. collective staffing challenges and inhibited collaborative pipeline investment.
- *A Dry Pipeline* (2024): CMO principals can no longer be as selective as historically — fewer candidates; they have expanded hiring preferences and used "upselling" tactics in job previews. **Most charter networks lacked reliable data on vacancies beyond individual schools or networks; even in cities where charters were ≥50% of enrollment, nobody could provide a sector-wide view of teacher or leadership needs.**
- CRPE conclusion: long-term recruitment/retention challenges are "too big for individual schools or charter networks to solve alone."

## Confidence note
The headline gap finding is **2017/18** (older). The 2024 *Dry Pipeline* study shows the data-blindness persists, so treat the *"the gap still holds in 2024/25"* framing as **Medium confidence** until a fresher US source is added. The *direction* (network-level data blindness) is well evidenced; the *currency* is the soft spot.

## Why it matters for ACME CO
A precise, evidenced **tension a four-country benchmark report resolves directly**: the buyers who operate at the largest scale (charter networks, district co-ops) are the ones flying blind on whether their own staffing pain is normal or fixable, because no one publishes the cross-network figure. ACME CO's proprietary placement data can *be* that figure — the "national/sector benchmark" upgrade the brief calls out.
